## chap2 Receivables

### Financial Assets Measured at Amortized Cost
Financial assets classified under this category must meet two specific conditions:
1.  Business Model: The asset is held for the purpose of collecting contractual cash flows.
2.  Cash Flow Characteristics: The contractual cash flows are solely payments of principal and interest (SPPI) on the principal amount outstanding.

Examples: Accounts Receivable, Notes Receivable, Other Receivables, and certain debt investments.

### Classification of Financial Assets
Financial assets are classified based on the business model for managing them and their contractual cash flow characteristics:
- **Amortized Cost:** held-to-maturity investments
    - *Basis:* Business model to hold and collect contractual cash flows; cash flows are SPPI.
    - *Accounting Accounts:* Loans; Accounts Receivable; Held-to-Maturity investments.
- **Fair Value Through Other Comprehensive Income (FVOCI):** available-sale investment
    - *General Case:* Business model to both collect contractual cash flows and sell; cash flows are SPPI.
    - *Special Case (Equity):* An irrevocable designation for non-trading equity instruments. Features: No impairment recognized; all transactions (except dividends) bypass the income statement and go directly to retained earnings.
- **Fair Value Through Profit or Loss (FVTPL):** trading investment
    - *Basis:* Financial assets that do not qualify for the above two categories (e.g., held for trading).

### 1. Notes Receivable

### 1.1 Overview
- **Definition:** A written promise (e.g., a bill of exchange or check) to receive a specific amount of cash on a specified future date, typically arising from the sale of goods or services.
- **Classification:**
    - **By Acceptor:** Commercial Acceptance Drafts, Bank Acceptance Drafts.
    - **By Interest:** Interest-bearing notes, Non-interest-bearing notes.
    - **Interest Calculation:** `Face Value x Interest Rate x Time`. interest rate change

### 1.2 Accounting Treatment for Notes Receivable

#### Initial Recognition
- **For a Sale:**
> Dr: Notes receivable (face value)
>> Cr: Operating revenue
>> Cr: Taxes payable – output VAT
- **To Settle an Existing Account Receivable:**
> Dr: Notes receivable
>> Cr: Accounts receivable

#### Subsequent Measurement (Accrual of Interest)
- At the end of an accounting period(like at year-end), interest accrued on an interest-bearing note is recorded.
> Dr: Notes receivable
>> Cr: Financial expenses

#### Collection at Maturity
- **For a Non-Interest-Bearing Note:**
> Dr: Bank deposits
>> Cr: Notes receivable (face value)
- **For an Interest-Bearing Note (No interest previously accrued):**
> Dr: Bank deposits (face value + total interest)
>> Cr: Notes receivable (face value)
>> Cr: Financial expenses (total interest)
- **For an Interest-Bearing Note (Interest partially accrued):**
> Dr: Bank deposits (face value + total interest)
>> Cr: Notes receivable (carrying value)
>> Cr: Financial expenses (interest for the current period)

#### Dishonored Notes (Overdue)
- When the payer fails to pay at maturity, the note is reversed and the amount due is transferred back to Accounts Receivable.
> Dr: Accounts receivable (face value + total interest)
>> Cr: Notes receivable (face value/carrying value)
>> Cr: Financial expenses (interest)

### 1.3 Notes Receivable Discounting
- **Definition:** Selling or pledging a note receivable to a bank before its maturity date in exchange for cash. The bank charges a discount fee.
- **Key Calculations:**
    - **Maturity Value:** `Face Value x (1 + Interest Rate x Time to Maturity)`
    - **Discount Interest:** `Maturity Value x Discount Rate x Discount Period`
    - **Discount Proceeds:** `Maturity Value - Discount Interest`
- **Accounting Treatment:**
    - **Without Recourse (Derecognition):** The risks of the note are transferred. The note is removed from the books.
	    - Dr: Bank deposit (proceeds) 
		- Cr: Notes receivable (carrying value, *face value+financial expenses*)
		- Cr: Financial expenses (difference, if proceeds > carrying value)
	- **With Recourse (Financing):** The company retains the risk. A liability ("Short-term loans") is recorded.
        - *At Discounting:*
	        - Dr: Bank deposit (proceeds)
	        - Dr: Financial expenses (difference)
	        - Cr: Short-term loans (*maturity value*)
        - *At Maturity (Payer pays bank):*
	        - Dr: Short-term loans
	        - Cr: Notes receivable (carrying value)
	        - Cr: Financial expenses (difference)
        - *At Maturity (Payment dishonored, company pays bank):*
	        - Dr: Accounts receivable
	        - Cr: Notes receivable
	        - Cr: Financial expenses
	        - *(Simultaneously, record payment to bank)*
	        - Dr: Short-term loans
	        - Dr: Financial expenses (difference)
	        - Cr: Bank deposit       
	    - *At Maturity (Payment dishonored, company cannot pay as well):*
	        - Dr: Short-term loans
	        - Dr: Financial expenses
	        - Cr: Short-term loans - overdue loans
	        - *(Simultaneously, record payment to bank)*
	        - Dr: Accounts receivable
	        - Cr: Notes receivable
	        - Cr: Financial expenses

### 1.4 Transfer of Notes Receivable
- **Without Recourse (e.g., Endorsement):** The note is derecognized.
	- Dr: Raw materials / Bank deposits etc.
	- Dr: Taxes payable - input VAT
	- Cr: Notes receivable
- **With Recourse:** This is treated as a collateralized transaction. The note is not derecognized, and a liability (e.g., "Accounts payable") is recorded for the purchase.

## 2. Accounts Receivable

### 2.1 Overview
- **Definition:** Amounts owed to a company by customers for goods sold or services rendered in the ordinary course of business.
- **Initial Measurement:** Recorded at the transaction price, considering trade discounts and cash discounts.

### 2.2 Accounting for Discounts
- **Commercial Discount:** A reduction from the listed price. It is not recorded separately; revenue is recorded at the discounted price.
- **Cash Discount:** An incentive for early payment (e.g., 2/10, n/30).
    - Accounts receivable is recorded net of the **estimated** cash discount.
    - At the balance sheet date, the estimate is reassessed. If the discount is no longer expected to be taken, the receivable and revenue are increased by the forfeited discount amount.
    - within the appointed period
	    - Dr: Bank deposit
	    - Cr: Accounts receivable (we believe the buyer can pay as appointed)
	- beyond
		- Dr: Accounts receivable
		- Cr: Operating revenue

### 2.3 Bad Debts (Allowance Method)
- **Concept:** China's accounting standards require the **allowance method**. The direct write-off method is generally not permitted.
- **Process:**
    1.  **Period-end Provision:** Estimate uncollectible amounts and record the provision.
	    - percentage of balance: provision for doubtful debt = ending balance of accounts receivable x provision rate
	    - aging of accounts
    2.  **Actual Write-off:** When a specific account is deemed uncollectible, write it off against the allowance.
    3.  **Recovery:** If a previously written-off account is collected, reverse the write-off and record the cash receipt.
- **Journal Entries (Allowance Method):**
    - **Recording the initial or adjusting provision:**
	    - Dr: Credit impairment loss
	    - Cr: Provision for doubtful debt
    - **Writing off a specific bad debt:**
	    - Dr: Provision for doubtful debt
	    - Cr: Accounts receivable
    - **Recovering a previously written-off account:**
	    - Dr: Accounts receivable
	    - Cr: Provision for doubtful debt
	    - Dr: Bank deposit
	    - Cr: Accounts receivable

### 2.4 Accounts Receivable Factoring (With Recourse)
- **Definition:** Pledging receivables as collateral for a loan. The company retains the risk of ownership.
- **Accounting:** The transaction is treated as a borrowing, not a sale. The receivable is not derecognized.
	- when borrowing
	- Dr: Bank deposit (cash received)
	- Dr: Financial expenses (fees)
	- Cr: Short-term loans / Notes payable
	- when returning
	- Dr: Short-term loans / notes payable
	- Dr: Financial expenses
	- Cr: bank deposit

### 2.5 Sale of Accounts Receivable (Without Recourse)
- **Definition:** Transferring receivables to a factor, who assumes the risk of collection.
- **Accounting:** The receivable is derecognized. A holdback may be recorded for potential returns or discounts.
	- Upon the sale
		- Dr: Bank deposit (cash received)
		- Dr: Other receivables – retained amount (estimated holdback)
		- Dr: Financial expenses (factor's fee)
		- Cr: Accounts receivable (full amount)
	- If the actual amount exceeds retained amount
		- Dr: Operating revenue
		- Dr: Taxes payable - output VAT
		- Cr: Other receivables - retained amount
		- Cr: Bank deposits
	- If the actual amount is less than retained amount
		- Dr: Operating revenue
		- Dr: Taxes payable - output VAT
		- Dr: Bank deposits
		- Cr: Other receivables - retained amount


## 3. Other Receivables and Prepayments
- **Other Receivables:** A miscellaneous category for receivables not covered elsewhere. Examples include employee advances, claims for damages, deposits, and amounts due from employees.
- **Prepayments:** Amounts paid in advance to suppliers for goods or services. They are assets representing the right to receive goods/services in the future.
	- employee advances
		- Dr: other receivables
		- Cr: cash
	- reimbursement
		- Dr: administrative expenses; taxes payable - imput VAT
		- Cr: other receivables; cash
	- deposit
		- Dr: other receivables
		- Cr: bank deposit

## 4. Disclosure
- **Balance Sheet Presentation:** Accounts receivable is presented at its net realizable value: `Gross Accounts Receivable - Provision for Doubtful Debt`.
- **Note Disclosures:**
    - Aging structure of accounts receivable.
    - Accounts receivable by customer category.
    - A reconciliation of the Provision for Doubtful Debt, showing beginning balance, current provisions, write-offs, and ending balance.

### 1. classification basis
An enterprise classifies financial assets based on:
- Business model for managing the assets
- Contractual cash flow characteristics (SPPI test: Solely Payments of Principal and Interest)

| Classification     | Business Model                                   | Cash Flow Characteristics | Account Name                               |
| ------------------ | ------------------------------------------------ | ------------------------- | ------------------------------------------ |
| Amortized Cost | Hold to collect contractual cash flows           | SPPI                      | Debt investments (HTM), Loans, Receivables |
| FVOCI (Debt)   | Hold to collect and sell                         | SPPI                      | Other debt investments                     |
| FVOCI (Equity) | Designation at initial recognition (irrevocable) | Not applicable            | Other equity investments                   |

### 2. Debt Investments (Amortized Cost)

Definition: Financial assets held to collect contractual cash flows consisting solely of principal and interest on specified dates.

#### 2.1 Initial Measurement
- Measured at fair value + transaction costs (excluding deductible VAT).
- Premium/discount recorded in “interest adjustment”.

Example – Premium Bond (PIP)  
Facts: Face value ¥800,000, purchased for ¥754,302, transaction costs ¥12,720 (incl. VAT ¥720).  
Effective interest rate = 6%. 

```
Dr: Debt investment – face value              800,000
    Taxes payable – input VAT                     720
    Cr: Debt investment – interest adjustment              33,698
        Bank deposit                                          767,022
```

区分折价发行的、每年付息的、到期还本付息的

#### 2.2 Subsequent Measurement – Effective Interest Method

Formulas:
- Interest receivable = Face value × Coupon rate
- Investment income = Amortized cost (beginning) × Effective interest rate
- ==Amortization = Investment income – Interest receivable==

Journal Entry (Premium Bond – PIP)  
```
Dr: Interest receivable                      40,000
    Debt investment – interest adjustment     5,978
    Cr: Investment income                              45,978
```

Journal Entry (Discount Bond – PIP)  
```
Dr: Interest receivable                      40,000
    Cr: Debt investment – interest adjustment             6,575
        Investment income                                 33,425
```

注：现金流是好算的，然后根据现金流去计算要求的收益率，没有得到的全部记作利息调整，然后因为这是我们计算的结果，所以在最后一期调整的时候我们就会得到完全的抵扣。

提前赎回依然按照原本的需求利率，在最后一起进行一并调整

#### 2.3 Impairment – Expected Credit Loss Model
- Recognize impairment when credit risk increases significantly.
- Present value of expected future cash flows discounted at original effective rate.

Example: Carrying amount ¥889,998, expected future cash flow ¥800,000 in 2 years at 6%.

```
Dr: Credit impairment loss                  178,001
    Cr: Provision for debt investment impairment         178,001
```

#### 2.4 Reversal of Impairment
- Reversal limited to original amortized cost (without impairment).

```
Dr: Provision for debt investment impairment
    Cr: Credit impairment loss
```

#### 2.5 Maturity – PIP
```
Dr: Bank deposit
    Cr: Debt investment – face value
```

#### 2.6 Maturity – MIP
```
Dr: Bank deposit
    Cr: Debt investment – face value
        – accrued interest
```

### 3. Other Debt Investments (FVOCI – Debt)

Definition: Financial assets held both to collect contractual cash flows and for sale, with SPPI cash flows.

#### 3.1 Initial Measurement
Same as amortized cost: fair value + transaction costs.

Example: Face value ¥80,000, purchase price ¥77,961.69 (incl. VAT ¥100), effective rate 6%.

```
Dr: Other debt investment – face value       80,000
    Taxes payable – input VAT                    100
    Cr: Other debt investment – interest adjustment         2,138
        Bank deposit                                          77,962
```

#### 3.2 Subsequent Measurement
- Interest income: using effective interest method (amortized cost).
- Fair value changes: recognized in Other Comprehensive Income (OCI).

Interest Entry (Year 1):
```
Dr: Interest receivable                      4,000
    Other debt investment – interest adjustment  672
    Cr: Investment income                                  4,672
```

Fair Value Entry (Year 1) – fair value ¥78,604, amortized cost ¥78,533:
```
Dr: Other debt investment – fair value change    71
    Cr: Other comprehensive income – FV change of ODI         71
```

#### 3.3 Impairment – FVOCI Debt
- Impairment loss recognized in P&L but recorded in OCI (carrying amount unchanged).

Example: Amortized cost ¥814,804, fair value ¥810,000, expected cash flow ¥800,000.

```
Dr: Other comprehensive income – FV change             4,804
    Cr: Other debt investment – fair value change                 4,804

Dr: Credit impairment loss                            14,804
    Cr: Other comprehensive income – impairment provision       14,804
```

#### 3.4 Disposal
- Difference between proceeds and carrying amount → Investment income.
- Accumulated OCI (fair value changes) transferred to Investment income.

```
Dr: Bank deposit
    Cr: Other debt investment – face value
        – interest adjustment
        – fair value change
        Investment income (balancing)

Dr: Other comprehensive income – FV change
    Cr: Investment income
```

### 4. Other Equity Investments (FVOCI – Equity)

Definition: Designated equity investments (non‑trading) measured at fair value through OCI.  
Key feature: Dividends go to P&L; gains/losses on disposal go to retained earnings (never recycled to P&L).

#### 4.1 Initial Measurement
- Fair value + transaction costs.
- Dividends declared but unpaid: recorded in “Dividend receivable”.

Example: 100,000 shares at ¥10/share + ¥2,000 transaction costs, ¥50,000 dividend declared.

```
Dr: Other equity investment – cost           1,002,000
    Dividend receivable                         50,000
    Cr: Bank deposit                                     1,052,000
```

#### 4.2 Subsequent Measurement – Fair Value Changes
Recognized in OCI.

Example: Fair value drops to ¥9/share, carrying cost ¥1,002,000.

```
Dr: Other comprehensive income – FV change    102,000
    Cr: Other equity investment – fair value change         102,000
```

#### 4.3 Disposal
- Difference between proceeds and carrying amount → recognized in OCI.
- Accumulated OCI transferred to retained earnings (not through P&L).

Example: Sold for ¥1,297,500, carrying amount ¥1,002,000 – ¥102,000 = ¥900,000.

```
Dr: Bank deposit                             1,297,500
    Other equity investment – fair value change 102,000
    Cr: Other equity investment – cost                1,002,000
        Other comprehensive income – FV change          397,500

Dr: Other comprehensive income – FV change    295,500
    Cr: Surplus reserve
        Retained earnings
```
